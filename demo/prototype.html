Explain the IFRS 17 discount rate methodology for insurance contracts and its impact on reported reserves
IFRS 17 requires insurers to discount future cash flows at a rate that reflects the time value of money and the characteristics of the insurance contract liability, rather than the insurer's own asset yields. There are two primary approaches: the top-down method, which starts with an observable portfolio return and strips out credit risk; and the bottom-up method, which begins with a risk-free rate and adds an illiquidity premium.

For long-duration contracts like annuities or whole-life policies, the choice of discount rate has a significant impact on the present value of the contract service margin (CSM) and loss component at initial recognition. Higher discount rates reduce reported reserves, while lower rates inflate them. Under the variable fee approach (VFA), used for participating contracts, the discount rate also affects the allocation of returns between policyholders and shareholders.

Volatility in discount rates flows through the P&L or OCI depending on the entity's accounting policy choice, creating potential earnings volatility that management often hedges with duration-matched fixed income instruments.
